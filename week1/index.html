Visualisations

  1. DR's visualisation of the Danish Local Election Results (or any other election data set -- the American presidential election is another good example) is an example of visualising a complex data set where different readers have different points of interest. Furthermore, they need to present actual raw data (vote tallies) alongside secondary metadata (e.g. who became mayor -- which is not a direct result of the election).

    • The coloured map gives a general overview of the election results by colouring communes by party colour with respect to the party color of the largest party in the commune.

    • For each commune, individual and party vote counts party seat counts, and actual elected councilmen alongside whoever ended up becoming mayor.

      This level is the most dense, and involves two visualisations.

      1. The map, where now polling stations are coloured by largest party,
      2. and the table window where everything about the results can be queried.

    • Finally, vote counts can be queried down all the way down to individual polling stations (but only by party).

    Positives

    • The map colouring allows for a brief overview of some of the general trends of the election. It can for example answer the question "where is the Conservative Party popular?".

    • It is very easy to find the results in a particular commune.

    Negatives

    • This map colouring could be misunderstood. Especially due to the fact that the party which is the largest does not necessarily reflect the political situation of a certain council.

    • The displayed tabular data is relegated to less that a third of the total space even though it is more complex and contains most of the data.

  2. League of Legends world championship 2017, second week group advancement visualization.

    Positives:

    1. Letting you grey out the outcomes that are no longer possible as the matches proceed.
    2. Structuring the team by team, since most people are only interested in the outcome for their favorite team.
    3. Using green, red and orange for the three different outcomes (classes).
    4. We have no scalar values, so no need for bars and spatial visualization.
    5. The small hints and explanations are good.

    Negatives:

    1. The greyed out outcomes continue to clutter the visualization after being removed out. Could be interesting if the design became more compact as outcomes are removed.
    2. Missing a win/lose number to each team that updates as the games proceed.
    3. Missing a visualization of which team has beaten which team, to give a better overview of the head-to-head of each team.
    4. Adding a % chance of advancement/tie-break/no-advancement for each team (assuming each match is 50/50) to get a quick overview.

Individual Parts: Visualisation ideas

William - Finding Correlations in the Previously Mentioned Election Data

The aforementioned visualisation of the election results doesn't delve much into aggregation. Leveraging past and present election data, it might be interesting to visualise trends, movements or correlations on a communal level. The data is available on KMD Valg (with a bit of scraping) and Danmark's Statistik.

For example, select two political parties (interactively) and take a scatterplot where the x-axis is the percent of votes cast for the first party and the y-axis the same for the second party. The results for each commune could then be plotted, perhaps with a shadow or trail displaying voter movement.

Henrik - Visualizing how the odds evolve in Tour de France

A visualization of how the bookmaker odds on different riders change as each stage of the race is completed.

Access: Getting access to the data would maybe prove difficult - you could replace bookmaker odds with race placements instead.

Story: You could add noteworthy events from the tour (example: "Alberto Contador wins a landslide victory in stage 4") and see how the odds/placements reflect that. The visualization would be an overview of the Tour, with the interactivity to explore different aspects such as: teams or specific riders, certain events (yellow jersey, polka dot jersey, top 3, etc), highlighting large shifts in odds/placement.

Audience: Bicycle fans and betting people.
