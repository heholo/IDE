Weather Change in Copenhagen!

Motivation

Our motivation is to show how it has gotten warmer during the last 100 year.

Method

The results we present below are gathered from the temperature data for Copenhagen found at nasa.gov. The dataset contains the monthly, seasonal, and meteorological means (see here) for the years 1880-2017 (although we skip missing data, so in practice we do not look at 2017).

For the temperature plot, we plot the yearly meteorological temperature mean as a function of year. Additionally we plot a least squares linear regression trend-line.

For the seasonal difference plot, we plot the difference between the summer (June, July, August) seasonal mean temperature and winter seasonal mean temperature (December, January, February) as a function of year. Again we plot a trend-line based on LLS linear regression.

Our charts are based on the Simple d3.js Graph tutorial, and the trend-line code is taken from skriv text.

Results

The yearly temperature plot above seems to indicate that it has gotten slightly warmer over the last 100 years. However, note that the data has high variability which is reflected in the low r2 value. Still, further evidence of increased temperature can be found in the list of coldest and warmest years below. We note that all but three of the 15 warmest years are after 1990 (inclusive), and likewise 1996 is the only year after 1990 which is among the 15 coldest years.

We also decided to investigate whether the discrepancy in the temperature between the seasons has changed. To investigate this, we plotted the difference in seasonal temperature of each year.

Looking at the chart, there does not seem to be a general trend in temperature difference between summer and winter. This is supported by a r2 value below 0.01 for our linear regression (and besides, the resulting slope is near non existent).

In conclusion, we believe we have observed that Copenhagen has seen an increase in temperature in over the last 100 years.
